How UCM Connects to SHM

Imagine a point mass moving at constant speed around a circle of radius A (the amplitude). Its angle at any time is θ(t) = ωt + φ, where ω is the angular frequency and φ is the initial phase.

x(t) = A · cos(ωt + φ)      y(t) = A · sin(ωt + φ)

If you shine a light from above and watch the shadow on the floor, that shadow oscillates left and right — that's Simple Harmonic Motion. The horizontal projection gives x = A cos θ and the vertical projection gives y = A sin θ.

SHM is the 1-D projection of Uniform Circular Motion

Top & Bottom of the circle:

  • Top (θ = π/2): y = +A (max displacement in vertical SHM), x = 0 (equilibrium in horizontal SHM). The vertical oscillator is at its peak; the horizontal one passes through the centre at maximum speed.
  • Bottom (θ = 3π/2): y = −A (min displacement in vertical SHM), x = 0 again. The vertical oscillator is at its lowest point; the horizontal one again passes through equilibrium.
  • Right (θ = 0): x = +A, y = 0 — horizontal SHM at max displacement, vertical SHM at equilibrium.
  • Left (θ = π): x = −A, y = 0 — horizontal SHM at min displacement, vertical SHM at equilibrium.
Period   T = 2π / ω    |    Frequency   f = ω / 2π

Adjusting A scales the circle (and oscillation amplitude). Adjusting ω changes how fast the mass orbits (and oscillation frequency). Adjusting φ rotates the starting position. Drag the mass on the circle to set any angle manually, or let it auto-run and watch the projections trace perfect sine and cosine waves.
